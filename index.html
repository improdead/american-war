Summary

Set in a future United States fractured by climate disaster and a second civil war, American War follows Sarat Chestnut from childhood in a drowning Louisiana to years inside a displaced-persons camp, where grief and propaganda reshape her into a living emblem of revenge. El Akkad borrows textures from real occupations, checkpoints, and drone warfare so the novel reads like reportage that has crossed into prophecy.

Story & stakes

“He had taught her long ago that when your country asks you to kill for it, your only question should be how high.”

Sarat’s arc asks how a child becomes a weapon, and whether any society that trades empathy for security can expect anything but cyclical violence. The novel refuses easy villains: every faction speaks the language of survival.

Main ideas from the essay angle

  • Thesis: When trauma is left untended, war reproduces itself across generations.
  • Subtopic 1: Climate inequality collapses civic trust before a single shot is fired.
  • Subtopic 2: Encampment life converts boredom and scarcity into radicalization pipelines.
  • Subtopic 3: Memory becomes either ethical witness or tactical payload, depending on who controls the archive.

The speculative frame is not escapism: it compresses timelines so readers feel how quickly “unthinkable” policies can normalize when fear is monetized.

About the author

Omar El Akkad is an Egyptian-Canadian novelist and journalist whose reporting from conflict zones informs the granular sensory detail of American War. His public talks stress that the novel’s horrors are recombinations of documented realities moved across geography and time.
