Summary: “The Drowning”

Set in a future United States shattered by climate disaster and civil war, American War follows Sarat Chestnut from childhood in a refugee camp to her transformation into a symbol of vengeance. The novel traces how violence, grief, displacement, and propaganda reshape identity across generations.

Story Content

“He had taught her long ago that when your country asks you to kill for it, your only question should be how high.” Sarat's life reveals how war distorts language, family, and memory until revenge feels like the only remaining form of agency.

This excerpted arc explains the novel's warning: conflict is inherited when societies abandon justice and empathy.

Key Themes

  • Thesis: War is cyclical when trauma is left unhealed.
  • Subtopic 1: Climate inequality drives political collapse.
  • Subtopic 2: Camps become engines of radicalization.
  • Subtopic 3: Memory can preserve truth or weaponize pain.

Rather than a simple dystopia, the novel mirrors real histories of empire, occupation, surveillance, and revenge to challenge how readers imagine “home” and “enemy.”

About the Author

Omar El Akkad is an Egyptian-Canadian novelist and journalist known for reporting from conflict zones and writing about displacement, power, and moral responsibility. His reporting background gives American War its documentary realism and ethical sharpness.
