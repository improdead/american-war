Summary

1

In a splintered United States gutted by climate failure and a second civil war, American War tracks Sarat Chestnut from a half-drowned Louisiana childhood into the disciplined cruelty of Camp Patience—and beyond, into the mythology of retribution.

El Akkad treats speculative fiction as a forensic lens: every drone shadow and ration line is stitched from real histories displaced onto American geography.

Setting & timeline

2

Fossil-fuel bans, coastal loss, and secessionist violence braid together until “security” becomes a word families mouth while packing go-bags.

Plot & pressure

3

Each relocation tightens the Chestnuts’ choices: stay lawful, stay human, or accept the camp’s arithmetic that pain must be banked until it can be spent.

Sarat’s mentors speak softly because they know volume is unnecessary when a child is already listening for orders.

Moral stakes

4
“He had taught her long ago that when your country asks you to kill for it, your only question should be how high.”

The novel asks whether revenge can ever be hygienic once it becomes a civic style—something citizens rehearse in language before they rehearse it in blood.

Climate & inequality

5

Climate grief is not backdrop; it is plot. Who owns high ground, who inherits mold and asthma, who pays for seawalls with their tax bills or their lungs—these questions decide which maps get redrawn first.

Remote war

6

Drones, checkpoints, and rumor networks translate occupation grammar into a domestic key. Readers recognize the choreography even when the zip code is fictional.

Camp Patience

7

Camps compress time: boredom becomes radicalization fuel; charity becomes surveillance; kindness becomes a ledger. Sarat learns to read power because she cannot stop staring at it.

Revenge cycles

8

The novel’s ethical spine is simple to state and brutal to practice: every act of violence mortgages the next generation’s peace. Trauma unprocessed becomes policy.

Thesis

9

Thesis: When societies choose vengeance over repair, war becomes a literacy taught at kitchen tables long before it is taught in training yards.

Your essay can defend, complicate, or historicize that claim using evidence from Sarat’s arc and from El Akkad’s interviews about journalistic method.

About the author

11

Omar El Akkad is an Egyptian-Canadian novelist and journalist. His reporting from conflict zones informs the tactile credibility of American War—the way bodies orient in heat, how checkpoints smell, how official language softens murder.

Empire Reversed: Postcolonial Readings of Omar El Akkad’s American War

In his novel American War, Omar El Akkad foresees an America that has lost the hegemonic power it once held over other countries. The United States is becoming a crippled, dependent, and extremely divided nation. From the postcolonial perspective, this transformation has great significance. Firstly, it forces readers to view America through the eyes of the colonized rather than those of the colonizer. The South becomes a place of refugee camps, drone attacks, foreign intervention, poverty, and political manipulation. Secondly, the Bouazizi Empire, composed of North African and Middle Eastern states, takes advantage of the American civil war. Postcolonial study of the novel reveals that America turns into a colonized state under the Bouazizi Empire, which exploits proxy war, imperialist policies, and the exploitation of Sarat’s pain to make victims imperialistic.

Starting with the proxy war element, it is important to note that El Akkad portrays America as an empire weakened enough to allow other empires to control it by extending conflict within its borders. The Bouazizi Empire did not intervene to conquer America in a conventional sense. Rather, the empire interferes in America’s affairs by giving Southern refugees assistance in the form of aid, weaponry, and political manipulation. This can be defined as proxy warfare, where one dominant country intervenes in the affairs of a lesser country by exploiting its internal conflict for its own gain. Primarily, Bouazizi aid seems humanitarian since it is aimed to help desperate Southerners through organizations like the Red Crescent. However, it is a form of politics meant to maintain division within America.

As noted by Russell Smith, American War is “a ‘turning-of-the-tables’ dystopia” (Smith). This is important because El Akkad depicts the reverse scenario of Middle Eastern states: here, a Middle Eastern–aligned power exploits America, which, traditionally, does the opposite. As Smith reports, Bouazizi agents interfere in American affairs by entering refugee camps and manipulating children to act as bombers, operating like “foreign intelligence officers” (Smith). This aspect corresponds to Sarat’s experience of Camp Patience. The camp is a place to watch displaced Americans, not merely their refuge. Thus, in this story, imperialism manifests itself in the form of “aid,” diplomacy, and political manipulation.

Additionally, a postcolonial analysis highlights that the power of imperialism lies in its ability to exploit pain. Sarat’s story proves beneficial for the Bouazizi Empire because of the damage the war has inflicted on her sense of home, safety, and identity. Having lost her father during childhood, being homeless in Louisiana, and then losing her mother in the Camp Patience massacre has led Sarat to become a refugee whose life will be completely defined by the war. Sarat’s grief allows others to capitalize on her vulnerability to instill in her a feeling of hatred against those who hurt her. Albert Gaines introduces Sarat to a simplistic ideology according to which the North is an enemy and therefore the division between “us” and “them” becomes clear. Sarat also engages in such simplifications when complaining about the difference between what is good for “them” versus good for “us” (El Akkad 156). Such simplification can be seen in colonial and imperial contexts since it involves othering the enemy who needs to be dehumanized to legitimize violence against them.

The argument proposed by Hasanul Rizqa further supports why Sarat should not be interpreted solely as a villain. According to Rizqa, Benjamin’s narration of Sarat emphasizes her status as a “war victim,” not as a mere genocide perpetrator (Rizqa 41). While Rizqa’s point does not necessarily excuse Sarat’s crimes, it explains how she was constructed as such. Her identity was not developed freely but, instead, through displacement, trauma, propaganda, and betrayal. Thus, the empire does not have to construct Sarat’s hate toward other nations; it only needs to direct her anger in the right direction, allowing Sarat to serve as a representation of imperialism built on wounded bodies and minds.

Finally, it is important to acknowledge how imperialism turns victims into perpetrators. In other words, Sarat’s suffering does not end after she reaches adulthood. After being caught by Northern forces, Sarat spends several years in Sugarloaf, which is reminiscent of many detention centers used by imperial powers throughout history. She develops such a fear of drowning that she is ready to believe even “no lie too big” (El Akkad 295). This moment is significant because it highlights how imperialism breaks victims, making them say and believe whatever they are made to. Sarat admits to killing someone she did not harm, proving the absence of justice in imperial politics.

However, when Yousef later returns to Sarat, he realizes that her trauma is useful for their cause. As Rizqa puts it, Yousef declares himself a representative of Bouazizi and explains that the Bouazizi Union wishes to intervene in U.S. affairs so that the country would no longer be a superpower (Rizqa 46). Thus, Sarat’s last mission is not her own at all. She believes she takes revenge on her country’s enemies, while her vengeance becomes a tool for Bouazizi imperialism. Sarat is a weapon that carries the virus inside the Reunification Ceremony. The tragedy is that Sarat acts not only out of hatred but out of genuine sorrow; yet the consequences benefit imperialists. El Akkad proves that the worst form of imperialism is when it convinces the oppressed to repeat the cycle of destruction.

The final page from Sarat’s diary makes the tragedy even clearer. Benjamin preserves only one page with Sarat’s memory of how she “was happy then,” before the war ruined her life and made her take revenge (El Akkad 379). In this way, readers are reminded that the protagonist was not always violent and aggressive; she lived in peace with her family and did not have to fight or kill anyone. The postcolonial significance of the novel lies in the fact that there was a “before” Sarat’s violent transformation. Empires often speak about the inevitability of violence, but El Akkad tries to prove that it is artificial and constructed by the system.

Thus, in conclusion, the reverse American future helps reveal the cruelty of imperialism in American War. First, proxy wars keep America unstable and dependent. Second, imperialism transforms sorrow into political violence through trauma. Finally, the Bouazizi Empire shows that victims can be transformed into weapons that ruin the lives of other victims. El Akkad creates a powerful novel not only because it evokes historical imperialism, but because it proves that empires adapt and survive by changing forms. The worst revelation is that empires do not merely conquer countries but also colonize pain, memories, and identities of the oppressed, who may become oppressors in response.
