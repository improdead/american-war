Summary

1

In a fractured America crushed by climate disasters and torn apart by a second civil war, American War follows Sarat Chestnut as she grows up. Her childhood in Louisiana is almost underwater, and when her family lands in Camp Patience, Sarat learns the brutal politics behind survival and revenge.

El Akkad uses speculative fiction like a microscope: he takes history we already know—drones overhead, waiting in ration lines—and drops it onto American soil in the future. The book covers about twenty years, from 2074 to 2095. The North bans fossil fuels, while the South—Mississippi, Alabama, Georgia, and what’s left of Louisiana and Florida—won’t let go. The war’s not shown in big, heroic sweeps. Instead, you see cramped rooms, nervous conversations, tired people just trying to get through another day.

key term Speculative fiction isn’t about trying to predict the future. It’s more about throwing real history into a new place, so it feels unfamiliar and forces you to look again.

Setting & timeline

2

The setting’s shaped by bans on oil, flooded coastlines, and violence as the South splits away. “Security” becomes just another word families say while grabbing bags and getting ready to run.

In three days, real floods hit 60,000 homes and forced 30,000 rescues. El Akkad never names that disaster directly, but he borrows its chaos for a future where storms like that keep coming and nobody expects the government to catch up.

image note That image? I picked it because the book’s first chapters talk about floodwaters creeping up to a second-floor porch. That kind of slow, relentless disaster—it matches.

Plot & pressure

3

Each time the Chestnuts relocate, their options shrink. Do they stay lawful, keep their humanity, or buy into the camp’s harsh belief that pain is a kind of currency, waiting to be spent?

Sarat listens carefully. Her mentors know they don’t have to shout—she’s already tuned in, waiting to be told what to do.

Moral stakes

4
“He had taught her long ago that when your country asks you to kill for it, your only question should be how high.”

The novel doesn’t answer the question about revenge. It just hangs there: Can revenge ever be clean, once a country turns it into routine, something you recite before acting it out for real? Sarat doesn’t give a speech—she gives you results. That quote above is as close as the novel gets to a motto, and it’s directed at a child.

reading note Watch how often the book disguises obedience as love. That’s the fuel.

Climate & inequality

5

Climate isn’t just scenery here—it’s the whole story. Who gets a dry house, who inherits mold and asthma, who pays for seawalls? Those questions decide which neighborhoods get erased. The Chestnuts live in a stilt house right by the water, relying on climate before bureaucracy for basics like food and a dry bed.

Here’s the feminist angle: When infrastructure fails, the real work of survival lands on families—and inside each family, it piles onto women.

Want nonfiction parallels? Look at Naomi Klein’s This Changes Everything or Rebecca Solnit’s A Paradise Built in Hell. They match up.

essay tip Don’t treat climate as just a backdrop. In American War, it’s the driver for every political move.

Remote war

6

Drones, checkpoints, and rumor networks—all the old tricks from war zones are adapted for home use. Readers recognize the patterns, even when the map’s made-up.

term card Remote war means fighting from afar: drones, sensors, contractors. Easy for the side launching it, devastating for people underneath.

Camp Patience

7

Camps distort time. Boredom turns into anger, kindness becomes currency, charity morphs into surveillance. Sarat studies power because, really, she can’t stop watching it.

Some of the book’s harshest moments happen in line. Waiting for water, shots, registration—waiting itself becomes a kind of politics. The longer you wait, the more the state tells you your time doesn’t matter.

Camp Patience doesn’t stand in for just one real camp. It borrows bits from everywhere—Lebanon, Bangladesh, Greek islands. You can see the similarities if you read some journalism about those places.

concept The camp is like a university, Sarat is its standout student, and Albert Gaines is the recruiter with the syllabus.

Revenge cycles

8

Here’s the simple but brutal truth in the book: every act of violence cripples the next generation’s peace. Trauma that isn’t dealt with turns into policy.

There’s a feminist layer: Men supply the ideology, women do the work to keep the household afloat, and “collateral” victims—usually women—are the ones performing unpaid care after the state walks away. The novel doesn’t shout this. It just keeps showing it so you notice.

theory hook Pair this with Cynthia Enloe on how militaries depend on invisible women’s labor, and with Judith Butler on whose lives are seen as grievable.

Thesis

9

El Akkad puts climate collapse and civil war right in front of us, showing it through Sarat—a girl who grows up in systems that turn care, pain, and survival tasks into weapons. He asks feminist questions: Whose bodies count as civilian? Whose anger makes sense? Who gets to control the story? These aren’t bonus details in geopolitics; they’re the foundations.

You can work Sarat’s story into feminist international relations (how militaries rely on invisible women’s work) and narrative ethics (who labels Sarat as monster, who calls her witness).

Here’s one way to build your paper: First, set up the gendered terms that feminist scholars use. Then, use three scenes from the book, analyzing them through that lens. Finally, argue that seeing Sarat’s violence as the system’s output—not an exception—changes everything.

thesis tip A strong thesis is one people will argue about. “Sarat is shaped by gendered violence” is too bland. Try: “The book says that making war less gendered is the key to ending it.”

About the author

11

Omar El Akkad is Egyptian-Canadian—a novelist and a journalist. He takes what he’s seen in conflict zones and brings it to American War, right down to how bodies move, how checkpoints smell, and how official language disguises violence.

He reported on Afghanistan, Omar Khadr’s trial, the Arab Spring, and Ferguson, all for The Globe and Mail. American War (2017) is his first novel. After that came What Strange Paradise, which won the Giller Prize. In 2025, his next book argues that “human rights” language in the West has quietly become a tool to excuse violence, not stop it.

good to know He literally drove through the U.S. South, sketching maps that rewind the coastline 6–15 feet below sea level. That’s how he built the geography for American War.

Portrait

12

Sarat Chestnut

13

She’s a fictional character—archival image below stands in for her, not as her biography.

She’s the main character. Her fierce curiosity is sharpened by her mentors—her journey tests whether empathy can survive constant propaganda. She’s a queer Black girl in a country that’s quit pretending it’s safe, raised near water and changed by the camp.

Focus on three traits: asking hard questions adults dodge, saving wounded things, refusing to flinch. Each one is survival, later used as material for her recruiters.

character note Sarat’s full name is Sara T. But she shortens it—politically. She’s rewriting herself before anyone else can.

Benjamin Chestnut

14

He’s Sarat’s brother and her opposite. He carries the hope for fixing things and throws Sarat’s choices into sharper relief.

Martina Chestnut

15

Mother—a model for survival ethics, grief, and boundary setting.

Martina is the clearest case of maternal care becoming political. She rations food, manages fear, translates danger into instructions a kid can follow, and keeps resisting turning her family into someone else’s soldiers. When she dies at Camp Patience, it isn’t just plot; it’s the loss of Sarat’s shield against recruitment.

feminist read Martina’s story is the best argument for care as resistance. When she’s gone, the last buffer vanishes.

Albert Gaines

16

Mentor—his knowledge comes across as care, but also as leverage.

Gaines is the classic calm male teacher, convincing a grieving girl that the world makes sense if she follows him. He looks like he’s just paying attention, but really, he’s recruiting. The book doesn’t let him off the hook, yet he’s not painted as pure evil—he genuinely believes what he says, which makes him dangerous.

foil Gaines is Martina’s opposite. She gives real care, no strings; he coats doctrine with concern.

Mothers, Monsters, and Archives: Gender, Violence, and Voice in Omar El Akkad’s American War

            

            

              

                Omar El Akkad’s American War is not “about” gender in the thin sense of occasional
                female characters; it is built from the feminist insight that modern war is assembled in
                kitchens, checkpoints, and refugee queues long before it is assembled in generals’ maps.
                By centering Sarat Chestnut—girl, daughter, sister, and eventually a figure the nation
                cannot decide whether to mourn or monster—El Akkad forces readers to ask who is permitted
                vulnerability, whose rage is treated as politics, and whose body becomes a public screen
                for other people’s ideologies (Enloe).
              

              

                A feminist reading begins with the obvious but easily neglected fact that Sarat’s life
                unfolds inside cascading failures of care: climate displacement, bureaucratic abandonment,
                and the normalization of lethal force as a substitute for social repair. Feminist critics
                have long argued that citizenship is lived through the body: who gets clean water, who
                carries children across checkpoints, who translates official threats for elders (Enloe).
                El Akkad literalizes that argument by making Sarat’s maturation coincide with the
                withdrawal of public protection; “security” arrives as drones, tents, and rumors, not as
                schools, clinics, or steady work.
              

              

                Martina Chestnut is more than backstory: she is the novel’s compressed image of maternal
                labor under siege—planning meals when calories are political, holding fear steady so
                children can sleep, translating violence into instructions a child can obey. When
                Martina dies in the massacre at Camp Patience, the loss is not only emotional plot fuel;
                it removes the primary buffer between Sarat and a world eager to recruit her grief. Read
                next to feminist theorizations of kinship under occupation, Martina’s arc shows how war
                privatizes survival work onto women and then punishes them for failing to be infinite
                (Enloe; El Akkad).
              

              

                Camp Patience itself reads as a gendered institution even when the text does not announce
                it with slogans. Encampment reorganizes time: boredom becomes discipline; queues become
                moral theater; aid becomes leverage. Women and girls disproportionately shoulder the
                hygiene, childcare, and emotional maintenance that keep camps from collapsing into pure
                anarchy—labor that rarely counts as “political” until it is too late (Enloe). El Akkad’s
                granular attention to Sarat’s coming-of-age inside that compressed social space makes
                feminist sense of how extremist politics can feel like intimacy when it is the only
                language that acknowledges your injury.
              

              

                Albert Gaines enters the novel as a familiar patriarchal type: the calm male mentor who
                offers clarity to a girl drowning in noise. His pedagogy trains Sarat to read the North as
                an undifferentiated enemy, tightening the “us/them” binary until it feels like oxygen. That
                scene of ideological seduction belongs in a feminist analysis because it shows how
                militarized masculinity often presents itself as rescue—even as it recruits girls into
                adulthood defined by hatred rather than choice (Butler). Sarat’s later repetition of what
                is good for “them” versus “us” marks how binary gender scripts of protection and revenge can
                harden into weapons (El Akkad 156).
              

              

                Judith Butler’s account of subject formation helps clarify why Sarat’s “choice” cannot be
                read as purely autonomous. Identities cohere through repetition under constraint; when the
                available scripts are narrowed to survival, martyrdom, or monstrosity, even rebellion can
                follow grooves carved by power (Butler). El Akkad refuses the cheap comfort of calling
                Sarat a spontaneous villain: her violence is cultivated in plain sight by institutions that
                prefer disposable daughters to expensive justice.
              

              

                Benjamin Chestnut’s archival narration raises a second feminist problem: who owns Sarat’s
                story once she becomes national myth? When testimony is edited, excerpted, and displayed,
                the line between witness and ventriloquism blurs. Hasanul Rizqa argues that Benjamin’s
                framing emphasizes Sarat as a “war victim” rather than reducing her to a flat figure of
                evil—an interpretive move with feminist stakes because it refuses the misogynistic
                pleasure of uncomplicated female monstrosity (Rizqa 41). Yet even sympathetic archives can
                discipline women’s pain into legible genres: the diary page, the confession, the lesson.
              

              

                Sugarloaf pushes these questions into the register of bodily sovereignty. Torture scenes
                in political fiction often risk spectacle; read through feminist theory, Sarat’s broken
                relation to truth—“no lie too big” she can be made to swallow—shows how domination targets
                not only minds but the felt integrity of the body (El Akkad 295). The novel’s ethical horror
                is not merely that states lie, but that they train victims to participate in their own
                erasure, rehearsing a gendered history in which women’s speech is discounted unless it
                confesses what power needs to hear.
              

              

                Caron E. Gentry and Laura Sjoberg’s work on women’s violence in global politics complicates
                easy headlines about “women terrorists.” Their framework asks us to separate spectacle from
                structure: when a woman strikes, commentators often treat her body as proof of civilization’s
                collapse rather than as evidence of long-running policy failures (Gentry and Sjoberg). Read
                through that lens, Sarat’s final mission is doubly tragic: she believes she is reclaiming
                agency, while multiple empires treat her trauma as a channel for their own ends—exactly the
                intersection of intimacy and geopolitics feminist international relations has traced for
                decades (Enloe; Rizqa 46).
              

              

                The preserved diary fragment—“she was happy then”—functions as a feminist counter-archive
                to nationalist mythmaking (El Akkad 379). It insists that Sarat had a before: not innocence
                as naïveté, but a life dense with ordinary attachments that war later converts into evidence,
                excuse, or propaganda. Feminist criticism has repeatedly shown how empires prefer women’s
                pain to be timeless and abstract (“womenandchildren”) rather than dated and political;
                El Akkad’s return to specific happiness is a refusal of that abstraction.
              

              

                None of this analysis requires excusing Sarat’s acts. Rather, it clarifies what a feminist
                paper can argue with rigor: that American War links climate crisis, civil war, and
                imperial gamesmanship through gendered pathways—through mothers who cannot shield daughters,
                through mentors who translate grief into doctrine, through narrators who decide which pages
                survive, and through a nation that learns to call a girl a weapon because it never learned
                to call her a citizen. El Akkad’s speculative reversal matters, finally, because it makes
                those pathways visible on American soil—where they were always already present.
