Summary

1

In a splintered United States gutted by climate failure and a second civil war, American War tracks Sarat Chestnut from a half-drowned Louisiana childhood into the disciplined cruelty of Camp Patience—and beyond, into the mythology of retribution.

El Akkad treats speculative fiction as a forensic lens: every drone shadow and ration line is stitched from real histories displaced onto American geography.

The novel spans roughly the years 2074–2095 of an imagined Second American Civil War. The North enforces a fossil-fuel ban; a holdout Southern bloc—Mississippi, Alabama, Georgia, and the half-submerged remnants of Louisiana and Florida—refuses. El Akkad treats the conflict the way reporters treat real ones: through small rooms, ration lines, and the language people use when they are too tired to be careful.

key term Speculative fiction: not prediction—a thought experiment that pulls real history out of its usual zip code so we’re forced to see it freshly.

Setting & timeline

2

Fossil-fuel bans, coastal loss, and secessionist violence braid together until “security” becomes a word families mouth while packing go-bags.

Real floods of 2016 inundated 60,000 homes in three days and forced 30,000 rescues. El Akkad doesn’t name that event; he loans its scale to a future in which storms like it arrive every season and the government has long since stopped pretending it can keep up.

image note I picked this photo because the novel’s opening pages describe water reaching the second-story porch—exactly this kind of slow, total drowning.

Plot & pressure

3

Each relocation tightens the Chestnuts’ choices: stay lawful, stay human, or accept the camp’s arithmetic that pain must be banked until it can be spent.

Sarat’s mentors speak softly because they know volume is unnecessary when a child is already listening for orders.

Moral stakes

4
“He had taught her long ago that when your country asks you to kill for it, your only question should be how high.”

The novel asks whether revenge can ever be hygienic once it becomes a civic style—something citizens rehearse in language before they rehearse it in blood.

El Akkad lets that question sit on the page without an answer. Sarat does not deliver a thesis statement; she delivers consequences. The pull quote opposite is the closest the novel gets to a creed, and it’s spoken to a child.

reading note Watch how often obedience gets dressed up as love in this novel. That swap is the engine.

Climate & inequality

5

Climate grief is not backdrop; it is plot. Who owns high ground, who inherits mold and asthma, who pays for seawalls with their tax bills or their lungs—these questions decide which maps get redrawn first.

The Chestnut family lives on a stilt-house at the edge of a drowned Louisiana parish; their access to clean food and dry beds is rationed by climate first and bureaucracy second. The novel makes a feminist point without slogans: when infrastructure fails, the labor of keeping people alive falls onto households, and inside households it falls onto women.

Compare with Naomi Klein’s This Changes Everything and Rebecca Solnit’s A Paradise Built in Hell for nonfiction parallels you can cite alongside El Akkad.

essay tip Don’t treat climate as setting. In American War it is the precondition for every political choice.

Remote war

6

Drones, checkpoints, and rumor networks translate occupation grammar into a domestic key. Readers recognize the choreography even when the zip code is fictional.

term card Remote war — war fought at distance: drones, sensors, contractors. Cheap for the launching state, total for the people on the ground.

Camp Patience

7

Camps compress time: boredom becomes radicalization fuel; charity becomes surveillance; kindness becomes a ledger. Sarat learns to read power because she cannot stop staring at it.

The novel’s most devastating sequences happen in line: line for water, line for inoculations, line to register. El Akkad treats waiting as politics—hours of waiting are how a state communicates that your time is worth less than its paperwork.

Camp Patience is not a metaphor for one specific real-world site; it borrows the grammar of many. Read it next to journalism on refugee camps in Lebanon, Bangladesh, or the Greek islands, and the choreography rhymes.

concept The camp is the novel’s university. Sarat is its star student. Albert Gaines is the recruiter who tells her the curriculum.

Revenge cycles

8

The novel’s ethical spine is simple to state and brutal to practice: every act of violence mortgages the next generation’s peace. Trauma unprocessed becomes policy.

The cycle has a feminist shape: men dispense the ideology, women keep the household alive long enough for the next round, and the people indexed as “collateral” are almost always the ones doing the unpaid care work that the state has abandoned. American War doesn’t say this in slogans; it just keeps the camera on it until you have to.

theory hook Pair this page with Cynthia Enloe on how militaries depend on women’s invisible work, and with Judith Butler on grievable lives.

Thesis

9

Thesis: In American War, El Akkad stages climate collapse and civil war through a girl who becomes a woman inside systems that weaponize care, grief, and survival labor—showing how feminist questions about whose bodies count as “civilian,” whose anger is legible, and who gets to tell the story are not accessories to geopolitics but its engine room.

Your paper can braid Sarat’s arc with feminist international relations (how militaries depend on women’s invisible work) and with narrative ethics (who frames Sarat as monster versus witness).

A workable structure: (1) introduce the gendered terms feminist scholarship has given us, (2) read three scenes through them, (3) argue what changes when we stop treating Sarat’s violence as an aberration and start treating it as the predictable output of the systems around her.

thesis tip Strong thesis = a claim someone could reasonably disagree with. “Sarat is shaped by gendered violence” is too tame. Try: “the novel argues that ungendering war is a precondition for ending it.”

About the author

11

Omar El Akkad is an Egyptian-Canadian novelist and journalist. His reporting from conflict zones informs the tactile credibility of American War—the way bodies orient in heat, how checkpoints smell, how official language softens murder.

He covered the war in Afghanistan, the trial of Omar Khadr, the Arab Spring, and the Ferguson protests for The Globe and Mail. American War (2017) was his first novel; What Strange Paradise (2021) followed, winning the Giller Prize. His 2025 book One Day, Everyone Will Have Always Been Against This argues that the West’s vocabulary of human rights has been quietly bent into a tool for excusing the violence it once condemned.

good to know El Akkad built the geography of American War by literally driving the U.S. South, sketching maps that take the coastline at minus 6–15 feet of sea level.

Portrait

12

Sarat Chestnut

13

Fictional character—archival portrait below is representational, not biographical.

Protagonist: fierce curiosity weaponized by mentors; her arc tests how empathy survives propaganda. She is a queer Black girl in a country that has stopped pretending to extend protection, raised by water and reshaped by camp.

Track three of her recurring traits: asking questions adults won’t answer, collecting wounded things, and refusing to flinch on command. Each one is a survival trait that her recruiters later read as raw material.

character note Sarat’s name is short for Sara T. The shortening is itself political—she rewrites herself before anyone can finish naming her.

Benjamin Chestnut

14

Brother and foil: carries the family’s hope for repair; throws Sarat’s choices into relief.

Martina Chestnut

15

Mother: survival ethics under collapse; grief reshapes her boundaries.

Martina is the novel’s clearest case of maternal labor as politics: she rations calories, manages fear, translates threats into instructions a child can obey, and refuses, again and again, to let her family be conscripted into someone else’s war. Her death at Camp Patience is not a plot beat; it is the loss of the only adult who was actively shielding Sarat from recruitment.

feminist read Martina’s arc is the strongest argument the novel makes for care as resistance. Lose her, lose the buffer.

Albert Gaines

16

Mentor figure: knowledge offered as care—and as leverage.

Albert Gaines is the recognizable archetype of the calm male teacher who tells a grieving girl that the world makes sense if she will only follow him into the next room. His pedagogy looks like attention. It is recruitment. The novel does not let him off the hook for that, but it also does not flatten him into villainy: he believes what he is saying, which is what makes it dangerous.

foil Gaines is Martina’s ideological opposite: she gives care without doctrine; he gives doctrine dressed up as care.

Motifs to trace

17
  • Water: drowning, baptism, washing evidence away.
  • Animals & augury: omens vs. superstition vs. realism.
  • Documents: letters, orders, rumors—who gets to archive truth?
  • Bodies: injury as text other characters misread on purpose.
  • Borders: checkpoints rehearsed first in conversation, then in concrete.
  • Names: who is allowed a full one; who is shortened by the state.
motif Water shows up at every emotional pivot. Mark every flood, every river crossing, every dampness on a wall. The pattern will write your paragraph.

Mothers, Monsters, and Archives: Gender, Violence, and Voice in Omar El Akkad’s American War

            

            

              

                Omar El Akkad’s American War is not “about” gender in the thin sense of occasional
                female characters; it is built from the feminist insight that modern war is assembled in
                kitchens, checkpoints, and refugee queues long before it is assembled in generals’ maps.
                By centering Sarat Chestnut—girl, daughter, sister, and eventually a figure the nation
                cannot decide whether to mourn or monster—El Akkad forces readers to ask who is permitted
                vulnerability, whose rage is treated as politics, and whose body becomes a public screen
                for other people’s ideologies (Enloe).
              

              

                A feminist reading begins with the obvious but easily neglected fact that Sarat’s life
                unfolds inside cascading failures of care: climate displacement, bureaucratic abandonment,
                and the normalization of lethal force as a substitute for social repair. Feminist critics
                have long argued that citizenship is lived through the body: who gets clean water, who
                carries children across checkpoints, who translates official threats for elders (Enloe).
                El Akkad literalizes that argument by making Sarat’s maturation coincide with the
                withdrawal of public protection; “security” arrives as drones, tents, and rumors, not as
                schools, clinics, or steady work.
              

              

                Martina Chestnut is more than backstory: she is the novel’s compressed image of maternal
                labor under siege—planning meals when calories are political, holding fear steady so
                children can sleep, translating violence into instructions a child can obey. When
                Martina dies in the massacre at Camp Patience, the loss is not only emotional plot fuel;
                it removes the primary buffer between Sarat and a world eager to recruit her grief. Read
                next to feminist theorizations of kinship under occupation, Martina’s arc shows how war
                privatizes survival work onto women and then punishes them for failing to be infinite
                (Enloe; El Akkad).
              

              

                Camp Patience itself reads as a gendered institution even when the text does not announce
                it with slogans. Encampment reorganizes time: boredom becomes discipline; queues become
                moral theater; aid becomes leverage. Women and girls disproportionately shoulder the
                hygiene, childcare, and emotional maintenance that keep camps from collapsing into pure
                anarchy—labor that rarely counts as “political” until it is too late (Enloe). El Akkad’s
                granular attention to Sarat’s coming-of-age inside that compressed social space makes
                feminist sense of how extremist politics can feel like intimacy when it is the only
                language that acknowledges your injury.
              

              

                Albert Gaines enters the novel as a familiar patriarchal type: the calm male mentor who
                offers clarity to a girl drowning in noise. His pedagogy trains Sarat to read the North as
                an undifferentiated enemy, tightening the “us/them” binary until it feels like oxygen. That
                scene of ideological seduction belongs in a feminist analysis because it shows how
                militarized masculinity often presents itself as rescue—even as it recruits girls into
                adulthood defined by hatred rather than choice (Butler). Sarat’s later repetition of what
                is good for “them” versus “us” marks how binary gender scripts of protection and revenge can
                harden into weapons (El Akkad 156).
              

              

                Judith Butler’s account of subject formation helps clarify why Sarat’s “choice” cannot be
                read as purely autonomous. Identities cohere through repetition under constraint; when the
                available scripts are narrowed to survival, martyrdom, or monstrosity, even rebellion can
                follow grooves carved by power (Butler). El Akkad refuses the cheap comfort of calling
                Sarat a spontaneous villain: her violence is cultivated in plain sight by institutions that
                prefer disposable daughters to expensive justice.
              

              

                Benjamin Chestnut’s archival narration raises a second feminist problem: who owns Sarat’s
                story once she becomes national myth? When testimony is edited, excerpted, and displayed,
                the line between witness and ventriloquism blurs. Hasanul Rizqa argues that Benjamin’s
                framing emphasizes Sarat as a “war victim” rather than reducing her to a flat figure of
                evil—an interpretive move with feminist stakes because it refuses the misogynistic
                pleasure of uncomplicated female monstrosity (Rizqa 41). Yet even sympathetic archives can
                discipline women’s pain into legible genres: the diary page, the confession, the lesson.
              

              

                Sugarloaf pushes these questions into the register of bodily sovereignty. Torture scenes
                in political fiction often risk spectacle; read through feminist theory, Sarat’s broken
                relation to truth—“no lie too big” she can be made to swallow—shows how domination targets
                not only minds but the felt integrity of the body (El Akkad 295). The novel’s ethical horror
                is not merely that states lie, but that they train victims to participate in their own
                erasure, rehearsing a gendered history in which women’s speech is discounted unless it
                confesses what power needs to hear.
              

              

                Caron E. Gentry and Laura Sjoberg’s work on women’s violence in global politics complicates
                easy headlines about “women terrorists.” Their framework asks us to separate spectacle from
                structure: when a woman strikes, commentators often treat her body as proof of civilization’s
                collapse rather than as evidence of long-running policy failures (Gentry and Sjoberg). Read
                through that lens, Sarat’s final mission is doubly tragic: she believes she is reclaiming
                agency, while multiple empires treat her trauma as a channel for their own ends—exactly the
                intersection of intimacy and geopolitics feminist international relations has traced for
                decades (Enloe; Rizqa 46).
              

              

                The preserved diary fragment—“she was happy then”—functions as a feminist counter-archive
                to nationalist mythmaking (El Akkad 379). It insists that Sarat had a before: not innocence
                as naïveté, but a life dense with ordinary attachments that war later converts into evidence,
                excuse, or propaganda. Feminist criticism has repeatedly shown how empires prefer women’s
                pain to be timeless and abstract (“womenandchildren”) rather than dated and political;
                El Akkad’s return to specific happiness is a refusal of that abstraction.
              

              

                None of this analysis requires excusing Sarat’s acts. Rather, it clarifies what a feminist
                paper can argue with rigor: that American War links climate crisis, civil war, and
                imperial gamesmanship through gendered pathways—through mothers who cannot shield daughters,
                through mentors who translate grief into doctrine, through narrators who decide which pages
                survive, and through a nation that learns to call a girl a weapon because it never learned
                to call her a citizen. El Akkad’s speculative reversal matters, finally, because it makes
                those pathways visible on American soil—where they were always already present.
